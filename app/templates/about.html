About This Dashboard

Getting Started

This dashboard provides a fast, open, and searchable interface for exploring PDF extracts of United States Supreme Court cases. You can search for any word or phrase, and the dashboard will instantly show you all matching opinions with context snippets. Click on any result to view the full PDF text, and use the dark mode toggle for comfortable reading at any time of day.

Built with open data, Python, Flask, and Bootstrap. Source code available on GitHub.

Proposal

title: getout-of-text3

subtitle: Developing a system of corpus and computational linguistic tools with application to administrative law debates

authors: Etienne P. Jacquot and Matthew Brook O'Donnell

institution: Annenberg School for Communication, University of Pennsylvania

abstract:

Questions concerning the clarity, ambiguity, reasonableness, and common-sense implicature of legislative language are not new but have recently taken center stage following a series of decisions by SCOTUS (West Virginia v. EPA, 597 U.S., 2022; Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo, 603 U.S., 2024). In the dissenting opinion of West Virginia v. EPA, Justice Kagan argued that the majority opinion's statement of the 'major questions doctrine' conveniently played a 'get-out-of-text- free' card', abandoning declared primary adherence to textualism (Scalia & Garner, 2012; Coppess 2022). Kagan similarly dissents in Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo, underscoring that in overturning Chevron, agency interpretations of statutory language will no longer receive judicial deference if “intervening in a 'major question,' of great economic and political significance” and as such fall prey to inconsistent and ideologically motivated applications of textualism.

The use of computational linguistic analysis in law, particularly with semi-automated approaches such as corpus linguistics (CL) has garnered some attention and adherents in part because it claims to help establish 'plain' or 'idiomatic' or 'ordinary' meaning and understanding of language empirically (see Solan, 2020; Goźdź-Roszkowski, 2021; Wright 2021). Valid critiques of the use of corpus methods on legal language have focused on the decontextualized nature of the applications which is surprising given that CL is defined by the focus on language-in-context (Bernstein, 2021; cf. Gries 2021).

Drawing on work in computational forensic linguistics, we describe the development of getout-of-text3, a system that brings together corpus (word frequency, KWIC concordance, keyword analysis, collocational profiles) and computational linguistic (narrative identification, graph representation and word embedding) tools with specific application to texts relevant to administrative law (specifically the cases and rulings in recent discussion of 'major questions doctrine'). This approach aims to address limitations of previous work (e.g., Crump, 2018) and take seriously the complexity of approaching questions of ambiguity and ordinary meaning within the framework of legal semiotics.
